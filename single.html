Education

How Education Changes
Everything for a Child

Amara Okonkwo
Executive Director
April 2, 2026
7 min read
3,241 views

When a child sits down in a classroom for the first time, something shifts — not just for that child, but for their family, their community, and the next generation that will follow them. At GSK Initiative, we have witnessed this transformation hundreds of times. And each time, it confirms what the evidence has always shown: education is the most powerful, lasting force for change we possess.

Over the past two decades working with street-connected children across West Africa, we have seen the full spectrum of vulnerability — children sleeping under bridges, children who have never held a pencil, children whose families believe schooling is a luxury they cannot afford. What has always struck us is not the scale of the problem, but the speed with which education begins to resolve it.

This post is about what we have learned, what the data tells us, and why your support of our education programmes creates ripple effects that last for generations.

The Scale of the Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is home to more than 98 million out-of-school children — the highest concentration anywhere in the world. In Nigeria alone, an estimated 10.5 million children of primary school age are not in school. Among street-connected children, the figure rises sharply: fewer than one in five have consistent access to formal education.

The causes are layered. Poverty forces families to prioritise immediate income over long-term schooling. Conflict displaces children from educational structures. Stigma keeps children who have experienced street life from re-entering mainstream classrooms. And inadequate infrastructure means that even when schools exist, they are often overcrowded, under-resourced, and out of reach.

What Happens When a Child Enters a Classroom

The first thing we observe — consistently, across communities — is a change in posture. Not metaphorically, but literally. Children who have spent months or years on the street carry themselves in a particular way: shoulders hunched, eyes down, always scanning for the next threat. Within weeks of joining our learning centres, we watch that posture change. The shoulders lift. The eyes meet yours.

This is not anecdotal. Our internal monitoring data shows that after three months in our programme, 84% of children report feeling safer, 79% say they feel respected by adults, and 91% express an intention to remain in school. These numbers matter because sustained attendance — what educators call "school persistence" — is the single most reliable predictor of long-term educational outcomes.

"Before GSK found me, I thought school was for other children — not for someone like me. Now I want to be a doctor. And I believe it is possible."
— Emeka, 14, Lagos State

Our Approach to Education

At GSK Initiative, we do not simply enrol children into existing schools and hope for the best. We recognise that children coming from street situations often have gaps in learning, unresolved trauma, and practical barriers — no uniform, no materials, no trust in institutions — that standard schooling does not address.

Our education model operates in three interconnected phases:

Phase 1: Transitional Learning Centres

Before entering mainstream school, children join a transitional learning centre where they receive literacy and numeracy catch-up classes, psychosocial support, and one-on-one mentorship. This typically lasts between two and four months and is designed to build the confidence and academic foundation they need to transition successfully.

Phase 2: School Enrolment & Material Support

We partner with local public schools to facilitate re-enrolment, covering the cost of uniforms, textbooks, and exam fees. Our field officers make regular school visits to track attendance, address problems early, and liaise between families, teachers, and the children themselves.

Phase 3: Mentorship & Long-term Follow-up

Enrolment alone is not success. We maintain follow-up contact with every child in our programme for a minimum of 24 months after school entry, providing mentors who meet with children monthly and respond to emerging challenges before they become dropout risks.

Programme Result: 2025 Cohort

Of 312 children enrolled in our 2025 education cohort, 243 (78%) remained in school after 12 months — compared to a national average retention rate of 51% for children who re-enter from street situations without support.

Beyond the Child: Community-wide Impact

The economic literature on education is unambiguous: each additional year of schooling increases an individual's earnings by 8–10% on average. For families living in poverty, this is transformative. When a child eventually enters formal employment — in a decade's time — they lift not just themselves but often multiple family members with them.

But the benefits arrive long before then. We have documented consistent patterns in our programme communities: families with children enrolled in education are more likely to access health services, more likely to engage in community savings groups, and less likely to experience domestic conflict. Education, it turns out, changes the social fabric of a household, not just the prospects of the child.

Did You Know?

UNESCO estimates that if all children in low-income countries left school with basic reading skills, 171 million people could be lifted out of poverty — equivalent to a 12% fall in global poverty.

How You Can Help

Our education programme costs approximately $180 per child per year to deliver — covering transitional learning, school materials, uniform, exam fees, and 24 months of mentorship follow-up. That is less than 50 cents a day to give a child a genuine shot at a different future.

Donations to GSK Initiative are tax-deductible and go directly to programme delivery. We publish annual audited accounts and impact reports, and every major donor receives a personalised update on the children their contribution has supported.

If you believe — as we do — that education is the most powerful investment we can make in a child's future, we would be honoured to have you alongside us.
